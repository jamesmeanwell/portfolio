“Instructional design is applied learning science. How can we claim to be scrutable in our approaches if we don’t track the underlying research, and can’t articulate how our designs reflect what is known?”

Quinn, C. (2021). Learning Science for Instructional Designers.

A lot of my work has quietly revolved around a simple question: what am I actually doing here—and why? That’s probably why Clark Quinn’s Learning Science for Instructional Designers landed the way it did for me. It cuts through a lot of the noise and reminds me that, even though design can take many forms, there’s a steady throughline: we’re always, in some way, applying what we know about how people learn.

The following four principles—as outlined in The Cambridge Handbook of the Learning Sciences—emerge from decades of cognitive psychology research and represent the core elemental-level mechanisms that drive individual learning:

  • Design for strategically spaced and repeated practice, with regular retrieval and application built into the flow of learning.
  • Manage cognitive load by sequencing content clearly and making connections across ideas explicit.
  • Create opportunities for learners to actively construct meaning through explanation, application, and revision.
  • Embed metacognitive moments that prompt learners to reflect on, evaluate, and adjust their thinking.

How do you—or how might you—intentionally design for these four elements in your own context, not as add-ons, but as part of the core learning experience?

Strategically regulated repetition and practice is really about making learning something that unfolds over time, not something that happens once and is done. Instead of treating each week as a standalone unit, you design cycles where learners are constantly pulling forward what they’ve already seen, applying what they’re working on now, and even getting a preview of what’s coming next. Low-stakes retrieval—quick checks, auto-graded quizzes, short prompts—does a lot of the heavy lifting, while instructor feedback builds on top of that rather than carrying the full load. The key is intentionality: revisiting ideas across weeks, not just within them, and creating a rhythm of practice that’s expected, supported, and sustainable, especially in asynchronous environments.

Managing cognitive demands while integrating information sources is about respecting the learner’s mental bandwidth. Every week should feel like a coherent step in a larger story: where we’ve been, what we’re focusing on now, and how it all connects moving forward. That means being selective—fewer resources, more thoughtfully connected—and helping learners make sense of those connections through clear structure, signposting, and synthesis. Things like weekly summaries, concept maps, or framing notes aren’t extras; they’re part of the teaching. In async settings especially, the way content is sequenced and organized becomes a primary indicator of teaching presence.

Engaging learners in the active construction of meaning shifts the focus from exposure to effort. Learning deepens when learners are asked to explain, compare, defend, and apply—not just share opinions or repeat information. This often means designing for a bit of struggle: letting learners try before showing them how, and using dialogue—especially time-delayed exchanges—to refine thinking over time. Instructional effort is most powerful when it targets key moments, like addressing misconceptions or modeling how to think through a problem. Tools and activities that make thinking visible—annotations, shared workspaces, peer critique—help move this beyond discussion into something more tangible.

Metacognitive awareness brings a layer of reflection that helps learning stick and transfer. It’s not just about what learners know, but how well they understand their own learning. Building this in means creating moments before, during, and after learning where learners predict, monitor, and evaluate their understanding. Simple moves—like asking for confidence ratings, prompting learners to compare those to actual performance, or asking what they’ll do differently next time—make feedback more actionable. Over time, this also normalizes conversations about learning strategies themselves, helping learners become more intentional and effective in how they study and engage.

My Approach to Learning Experience Design

Each project I work on is unique in terms of its goals, stakeholders, and constraints; however, the same areas of practice do emerge, which I see as instructional design, user experience design, content development, and project management. Learning experience design is inherently interdisciplinary, drawing from multiple fields that shape how people learn, interact, and make meaning. While all four areas inform my work, the sections that follow focus specifically on instructional design and user experience design, as these most directly shape how learning is structured and experienced.

Instructional Design

Working as a teacher trainer/educator early in my career indelibly forged the way I practice instructional design. The challenge was to help adult learners develop foundational language teaching and classroom management competencies in a four-week crash course to teaching. This course was designed by the University of Cambridge and implemented at centres globally either as an in-person or blended delivery modality.

Over the span of eight years (2013–2021), I saw firsthand how successful their experiential learning model was, which was essentially an iterative loop of input, practice, expert feedback, and reflection. My role was to provide daily workshops through which the teachers-in-training could learn the knowledge and skills related to language learning and language analysis. Lectures were eschewed in favour of hands-on, guided-discovery exercises designed to make connections between what we know about how adults learn a language and how we, as teachers, can best leverage this knowledge through intentionally designed activities in the classroom.

While the hands-on approach was undoubtedly effective, the real magic happened in the experiential learning component of the course; that is, through actually teaching adult language learners. My role during the teaching practicum was to observe each lesson and then provide written and verbal feedback to each teacher-in-training. Many of the methods from what I know now as cognitive apprenticeship were employed to promote the development of expertise such as modelling, coaching, scaffolding, and reflection.

Now, as a learning experience designer at McGill University (2021–present), not only is each project different, but the point at which I join a project also varies. For example, some projects are a modification to an existing course within an established program. Others involve taking a granular look at a proposed program's curriculum. Regardless of the project phase in which I find myself, one of the first questions I ask is about the learners: who are they, and to what extent are we trying to target a change in their knowledge, skills, and/or values? This focus on what learners need to do and become naturally extends beyond content and into how they experience the process of learning itself.

User Experience Design

It’s at this point that user experience design becomes essential. Learning from other design fields has been critical to shaping how I think about learning experience design, and I'd like to share a few short examples. Don Norman (2013) in The Design of Everyday Things talks about the need to design for both a higher-level activity and its lower-level tasks:

Apple’s success with its music player, the iPod, was because Apple supported the entire activity involved in listening to music: discovering it, purchasing it, getting it into the music player, developing playlists (that could be shared), and listening to the music. Apple also allowed other companies to add to the capabilities of the system with external speakers, microphones, all sorts of accessories. Apple made it possible to send the music throughout the home, to be listened to on those other companies’ sound systems. Apple’s success was due to its combination of two factors: brilliant design plus support for the entire activity of music enjoyment. (p. 233)

So how does product design translate to learning experience design? Well, an online course is a product that someone buys. And if we imagine learning or studying as the activity, then we need to design support to aid its completion. In other words, instructional design may define what needs to be learned, but user experience design shapes how that learning actually unfolds in practice.

Let's say a student is taking a course in web design. Conventional practice might be limited to thinking about the instructional design of the course. For instance, articulating desired results, deciding what would be acceptable evidence of achievement of those results, and then creating or curating appropriate learning material. In more concrete terms, we could say the end result would be that we want students to build a single-page website. The acceptable evidence of their achievement of the end result would be, stay with me, to design a single-page website. With respect to appropriate learning material, select which elements of HTML and CSS the students would need to learn and that's a pretty good start to shaping the instruction.

But where in that process do we think about the activity? For example, if students are required to watch a video tutorial on web design while at the same time following along in their own text editor, wouldn't that be rather clunky? Toggling back and forth between windows and pausing the video repeatedly? Splitting windows half-screen but then losing readability? And what if they want to take notes during all of this? The small tasks involved in the larger activity of learning start to add up and can quickly diminish one's reservoir of goodwill if not handled gracefully. These are not edge cases—they are the experience of learning.

Ellen Lupton (2017), in Design Is Storytelling, describes how designers Aaron Walter and Jared M. Spool took Maslow's hierarchy of needs and repurposed it for a user experience design context, which shows a progression from functional, reliable, usable, and, on top, pleasurable (p. 64). What’s useful here is how closely this maps to the earlier idea of designing for both the activity and its component tasks.

If the small, moment-to-moment interactions of learning—watching, note-taking, switching tools, applying concepts—aren’t at least functional and reliable, the larger activity of learning begins to break down. And if they’re not usable, learners end up spending more effort managing the experience than engaging with the content itself. But when those layers are handled well, something shifts: the experience can move beyond friction and start to feel fluid, even enjoyable. This is where learning experience design becomes more than structuring content—it becomes the careful orchestration of an activity, where reducing friction at the task level makes deeper engagement at the learning level possible.

Taken together, instructional design and user experience design are not separate layers but complementary lenses—one shaping the learning goals and structure, the other ensuring those intentions can actually be realized by learners in practice.
