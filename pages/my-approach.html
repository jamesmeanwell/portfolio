“Instructional design is applied learning science. How can we claim to be scrutable in our approaches if we don’t track the underlying research, and can’t articulate how our designs reflect what is known?”

Quinn, C. (2021). Learning Science for Instructional Designers.

A lot of my work has quietly revolved around a simple question: what am I actually doing here—and why? That’s probably why Clark Quinn’s Learning Science for Instructional Designers landed the way it did for me. It cuts through a lot of the noise and reminds me that, even though design can take many forms, there’s a steady throughline: we’re always, in some way, applying what we know about how people learn. From that perspective, a few core ideas start to feel less like abstract theory and more like practical anchors for design, as outlined in The Cambridge Handbook of the Learning Sciences:

  • Design for strategically spaced and repeated practice, with regular retrieval and application built into the flow of learning.
  • Manage cognitive load by sequencing content clearly and making connections across ideas explicit.
  • Create opportunities for learners to actively construct meaning through explanation, application, and revision.
  • Embed metacognitive moments that prompt learners to reflect on, evaluate, and adjust their thinking.

Interdisciplinarity

Each project I work on is unique in terms of its goals, stakeholders, and constraints; however, the same areas of practice do emerge, which I see as instructional design, user experience design, content development, and project management.

Instructional Design

Working as a teacher trainer/educator early in my career indelibly forged the way I practice instructional design. The challenge was to help adult learners develop foundational language teaching and classroom management competencies in a four-week crash course to teaching. This course was designed by the University of Cambridge and implemented at centres globally either as an in-person or blended delivery modality.

Over the span of eight years (2013–2021), I saw firsthand how succesful their experiential learning model was, which was essentially an interative loop of input, practice, expert feedback, and reflection. My role was to provide daily workshops through which the teachers-in-training could learn the knowledge and skills related to language learning and language analysis. Lectures were eschewed in favour of hands-on, guided-discovery exercises designed to make connections between what we know about how adults learn a language and how we, as teachers, can best leverage this knowledge through intentionally designed activities in the classroom.

While the hands-on approach was undoubtedly effective, the real magic happened in the experiential learning component of the course; that is, through actually teaching adult language learners. My role during the teaching practicum was to observe each lesson and then provide written and verbal feedback to each teacher-in-training. Many of the methods from what I know now as cognitive apprenticeship were employed to promote the development of expertise such as modelling, coaching, scaffolding, and reflection.

Now, as a learning experience designer at McGill University (2021–present), not only is each project different, but the point at which I join a project also varies. For example, some projects are a modification to an existing course within an established program. Others involve taking a granular look at a proposed program's curriculum. Regardless of the project phase in which I find myself, one of the first questions I ask is about the learners: who are they, and to what extent are we trying to target a change in their knowledge, skills, and/or values?

User Experience Design

Learning from other design fields has been critical to shaping how I think about learning experience design, and I'd like to share a few short examples. Don Norman (2013) in The Design of Everyday Things talks about the need to design for both a higher-level activity and its lower-level tasks:

Apple’s success with its music player, the iPod, was because Apple supported the entire activity involved in listening to music: discovering it, purchasing it, getting it into the music player, developing playlists (that could be shared), and listening to the music. Apple also allowed other companies to add to the capabilities of the system with external speakers, microphones, all sorts of accessories. Apple made it possible to send the music throughout the home, to be listened to on those other companies’ sound systems. Apple’s success was due to its combination of two factors: brilliant design plus support for the entire activity of music enjoyment. (p. 233)

So how does product design translate to learning experience design? Well, an online course is a product that someone buys. And if we imagine learning or studying as the activity, then we need to design support to aid its completion.

Let's say a student is taking a course in web design. Conventional practice might be limited to thinking about the instructional design of the course. For instance, articulating desired results, deciding what would be acceptable evidence of achievement of those results, and then creating or curating appropriate learning material. In more concrete terms, we could say the end result would be that we want students to build a single-page website. The acceptable evidence of their achievement of the end result would be, stay with me, to design a single-page website. With respect to appropriate learning material, select which elements of HTML and CSS the students would need to learn and that's a pretty good start to shaping the instruction.

But where in that process do we think about the activity? For example, if students are required to watch a video tutorial on web design while at the same time following along in their own text editor, wouldn't that be rather clunky? Toggling back and forth between windows and pausing the video repeatedly? Splitting windows half-screen but then losing readability? And what if they want to take notes during all of this? The small tasks involved in the larger activity of learning start to add up and can quickly diminish one's reservoir of goodwill if not handled gracefully.

Ellen Lupton (2017), in Design Is Storytelling, describes how designers Aaron Walter and Jared M. Spool took Maslow's hierarchy of needs and repurposed it for a user experience design context, which shows a progression from functional, reliable, usable, and on top, pleasurable (p. 64).
